# Scope Agreement: Getting Everyone on the Same Page

> **For business stakeholders.** This doc helps you align with leaders (Operations, Finance, others) who aren’t in the weeds day to day. The goal: one clear agreement on what we’re doing, what we’re not doing, and what success looks like—so nobody is surprised later.

---

## The right document to give business reps

The standard document for this is a **Project Scope Statement (one-page)** or **Scope of Work (SOW) one-pager**. It has three main parts: (1) a short description of the initiative, (2) what’s in scope, (3) what’s out of scope, plus a **sign-off** so they formally agree on the MVP and you move forward.

**Use this one-pager with your business reps:**  
**→ [Product/MVP-Scope-Agreement-onepager.md](MVP-Scope-Agreement-onepager.md)**

Print or share that single page. They review the initiative, in scope, and out of scope; they sign; then you proceed. The rest of this file is a guide for how to use and fill that document.

---

## What You’re Really Agreeing On

When you sit down with stakeholders to agree on a project, you’re answering a few simple questions. Here’s what you bring to the table and why it matters to the business:

| What you share | In plain terms | Why it matters to them |
|----------------|----------------|-------------------------|
| **What’s in, what’s out** | A clear list: what we *are* delivering in this phase, and what we are *not*. | Everyone leaves with the same picture. No “I thought we were doing X.” |
| **Why we’re doing it** | The business reason: who’s affected, what problem we’re solving, and why now. | Helps them support the project and prioritize it with other work. |
| **How we’ll know it worked** | A few concrete outcomes or metrics we’ll use to say “this succeeded.” | They can hold the team accountable and report up with confidence. |
| **When things happen** | Key dates (e.g. when we start, when we pilot, when we go live). | They can plan their side (training, rollout, support) and set expectations. |
| **Who does what** | Who is responsible for delivery, rollout, approvals, and support. | No confusion about ownership. |
| **What we need from them** | Approvals, decisions, or inputs you need from their team—and any limits (budget, people). | They can commit or escalate early instead of being surprised. |
| **What we’re not doing (yet)** | Things that are explicitly *not* in this phase, so expectations are clear. | Avoids “I assumed we were doing X” and keeps the project focused. |
| **Sign-off** | Names, roles, and a simple “approved” so there’s a single record of agreement. | One place that says: “We all agreed to this.” |

---

## Where This Gets Written Down

- The main place is usually the **product requirements document** (the doc that defines what we’re building and why). It holds the “in scope / out of scope” list, timeline, roles, and the sign-off table.
- Some teams also use a short **scope of work** (a one- or two-pager) for a quick handshake with stakeholders. Use whatever your organization already does for internal agreements.

---

## One-Pager for the Meeting

Use this when you’re in the room with stakeholders. **The “out of scope” section is filled on purpose**—so everyone explicitly agrees on what we’re *not* doing in this phase.

```
PROJECT: [e.g. Ikon Lot Scan]
WHO WE’RE MEETING: [e.g. VP Operations]

We’re asking you to agree to the following:

1. WHAT WE’RE DELIVERING (this phase)
   • See Product/Stakeholder.md for the full list.
   • [Or paste a short summary here.]

2. WHAT WE’RE NOT DELIVERING (this phase)
   • [Item A] — [e.g. “Dealer self-audits — not in this phase; we can revisit later.”]
   • [Item B] — [e.g. “Rollout to all locations — we’re starting with a pilot first.”]
   • [Item C] — [e.g. “Feature X — deferred to a later phase.”]
   • Only add items you’ve actually agreed are out of scope. Don’t add guesses.

3. WHAT WE NEED FROM YOU
   • [e.g. Approve the rollout plan by DATE]
   • [e.g. No new scope without going through a change process]

4. HOW WE’LL MEASURE SUCCESS
   • [e.g. Metric 1], [Metric 2], by [Date]

5. KEY DATES
   • [e.g. Pilot starts]: [Date]
   • [e.g. Go-live]: [Date]
```

After the meeting, update the main agreement doc with any changes and send it around so the agreement is on the record.

---

## “Out of Scope”—Why We Spell It Out

- **Why we do it:** So everyone agrees on what we’re *not* building. That way nobody says later, “I thought we were doing X.”
- **Rule of thumb:** Only list things you’ve actually agreed are out of scope. Don’t invent a list.
- **Where to put it:** In the “Out of scope” section of your main requirements doc, or in the one-pager above, or in Product/Stakeholder.md if you add that section there.
- **How to say it:** Keep it simple. For example: “We are not doing X in this phase” or “X is out of scope for this release.”
- **If nothing is agreed yet:** Leave the list empty or write “To be defined with stakeholders.” Don’t add items you haven’t agreed on.

---

## Tips for a Smooth Agreement

- **Bring them in early.** Include key stakeholders in the conversation before the agreement is final. They’re partners, not just signers.
- **Be clear about what’s out.** Saying “We are not doing X in this phase” up front prevents surprises later.
- **Keep one source of truth.** Use one doc (or one place) for the agreement. Avoid relying only on slides or verbal nods.
- **Update when things change.** If scope or dates change, realign with stakeholders and update the sign-off.
